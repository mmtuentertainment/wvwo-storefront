# Elk River Wildlife Management Area: Historical and Cultural Research Brief

**Location and Management:** The **Elk River Wildlife Management Area (Elk River WMA)** is a 18,396-acre to 19,646-acre property located in **Braxton County, West Virginia**, east of Sutton, managed by the West Virginia Division of Natural Resources (WVDNR). The area encompasses portions of the Elk River and Holly River watersheds upstream of Sutton Lake, which was created by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers between 1956 and 1961.[^1][^2][^3][^4]

## A. Timeline: Elk River WMA's Evolution

### Pre-Contact \& Early European Settlement (1600s–1800s)

- **1600s–1700s:** The Elk River Valley and surrounding lands were used as **seasonal hunting grounds by the Shawnee**, Delaware (Lenape), and Mingo peoples, who migrated to the region after displacement by the Iroquois Confederacy. The Delaware "had several autonomous settlements as far south as present-day Braxton County." The Elk River's name is believed to derive from the Shawnee designation as "river of plenty fat elk" according to historian Roy Bird Cook.[^5][^6][^7]
- **1774:** Following Lord Dunmore's War and the Treaty of Camp Charlotte, the Shawnee were compelled to recognize the Ohio River as their southern boundary, effectively relinquishing hunting claims to lands in present-day West Virginia and Kentucky.[^6]
- **1836:** Braxton County was officially created from portions of Lewis, Kanawha, and Nicholas counties on January 15, 1836.[^8]


### Industrial Era: Logging Boom (1880–1930)

- **1880–1925:** The **logging peak occurred from 1902 to 1925**, with intensive timber extraction throughout Braxton County and the Elk River basin. The Pardee and Curtin Lumber Company operated logging camps and mills in Braxton, Nicholas, and Webster counties, including the Sutton Mills, with operations documented from at least 1898–1924.[^9][^10]
- **1894:** A **large band mill was established at the mouth of Holly River** by the Palmer Lumber Company, bringing logs from extensive timber holdings across the region.[^11]
- **1879–1920:** Between 1879 and 1920, there was a "great logging boom" with hundreds of sawmills operating across 30 counties, removing approximately **30 billion board feet of timber** and leaving the landscape devastated by subsequent fires, erosion, and soil degradation.[^12]


### Early Wildlife Management \& Conservation Milestones (1911–1945)

- **1911:** Following the purchase of the **first sections of the Monongahela National Forest** by the federal government, six refuges (called "game breeding areas") were established on these lands, primarily to protect wild turkey.[^9]
- **1913:** **Fifty (50) Rocky Mountain elk** were obtained from Yellowstone National Park by the State of West Virginia and transferred to an enclosure maintained by the Allegheny Sportsmen's Association at Minnehaha Springs in Pocahontas County; they were subsequently released into surrounding mountains. This represents the **first documented game animal importation by West Virginia**.[^13][^14][^9]
- **1915:** The **West Virginia Legislature passed a law** authorizing the Forest, Game and Fish Warden to set aside tracts of land "to be used as refuges for wild game and birds and on which no hunting shall be allowed."[^9]
- **1922–1923:** The Game and Fish Commission established the **first state-owned game refuge: Seneca State Forest and Game Refuge** (10,487 acres of cut-over forest land near Marlinton, Pocahontas County). Other purchases in the 1920s included lands that later became **Nathaniel Mountain and Short Mountain Wildlife Management areas, Kumbrabow State Forest, and Holly River and Watoga State parks.**[^9]
- **1938:** **Holly River was designated as a wildlife refuge** and opened as an official state park, though it was not deeded to the state by the federal government until 1954.[^15]
- **1945:** A **memorandum of understanding** was signed between the West Virginia Conservation Commission and the U.S. Forest Service, establishing a unified wildlife management approach on 303,600 acres of the Monongahela National Forest, divided into 10 individual management areas with assigned resident wildlife managers.[^9]


### Dam Construction \& Modern Recreation Era (1956–1961)

- **1938:** Sutton Dam was authorized by Congress in the **Flood Control Act of 1938.**[^4]
- **1956–1961:** **Construction of Sutton Dam** took place, completed in 1961 at a cost of \$35 million, creating Sutton Lake (1,520 acres, 1,440 acres within the WMA section). The dam was built primarily for flood control on the Elk, Kanawha, and Ohio Rivers and has prevented over \$375 million in flood damages since completion.[^16][^17][^4]


### Modern Wildlife Management \& Renaming (1989–Present)

- **1989:** **"Game refuge" terminology was replaced with "Wildlife Management Areas"** statewide, reflecting expanded roles beyond hunting refuge status to include diverse wildlife recreation and management.[^18]
- **2015:** The **West Virginia Legislature passed legislation** establishing an official Elk Management Area and tasking the WVDNR with developing an active elk restoration program.[^14]
- **2016–Present:** Modern **elk restoration efforts** have resulted in the reintroduction of Rocky Mountain elk to West Virginia for the first time since the 1870s–1890s.[^19][^20]

## B. "How It Became a WMA": Narrative Account

The Elk River Wildlife Management Area emerged from West Virginia's transition from unmanaged exploitation to deliberate conservation planning during the 20th century. For millennia before European colonization, the Elk River Valley and surrounding lands served the Shawnee, Delaware, and Mingo peoples as hunting grounds rich in elk, deer, and other game. The river's Shawnee name, "river of plenty fat elk," reflected the abundance of this apex herbivore. However, European settlement and the treaties of the 1770s—particularly the Treaty of Camp Charlotte (1774)—displaced these Indigenous nations from their ancestral hunting territories.[^7][^5][^6]

The landscape experienced catastrophic transformation during the 19th and early 20th centuries. From 1879 to 1920, industrial logging removed approximately **30 billion board feet of timber**, with the Palmer Lumber Company operating a major band mill at the mouth of Holly River beginning in 1894 and the Pardee and Curtin Lumber Company working extensive operations throughout Braxton County through the 1920s. This logging boom, combined with uncontrolled wildfires that followed timber harvesting, eliminated nearly all old-growth forest in the region by approximately 1928, fundamentally altering habitat and wildlife populations.[^10][^12][^11][^9]

Recognizing this ecological crisis, West Virginia lawmakers began establishing a formal conservation infrastructure in the early 1900s. After the federal government purchased the first sections of the Monongahela National Forest in 1911, state authorities followed suit. The **1923 purchase of Seneca State Forest and Game Refuge** marked the first state-owned conservation area and catalyzed a series of acquisitions throughout the 1920s, including Holly River (designated a wildlife refuge in 1938). These early refuges prioritized **wild turkey restoration**, which had been hunted to near-extinction, and implemented "controlled public hunting when needed" for species such as deer.[^15][^9]

The area's modern configuration was shaped by Sutton Dam's construction (1956–1961), which created an 1,440-acre impoundment within the WMA boundaries, providing flood control while enabling recreational fishing and boating. By the mid-20th century, **cooperative agreements between the WVDNR and the U.S. Forest Service (signed in 1945)** had unified management of over 300,000 acres into structured wildlife management areas with dedicated resident wildlife managers.[^16][^9]

The formal designation "Elk River Wildlife Management Area" reflects this consolidated management philosophy, though precise records of when this specific property received its current name remain unclear from available sources (Unverified). What is documented is that **in 1989, West Virginia redesignated all "game refuges" as "Wildlife Management Areas"** to reflect their expanded mission beyond hunting refuge status to encompass comprehensive wildlife and habitat management for all citizen recreation and ecological benefit.[^18]

## C. Cultural Context: Indigenous Stewardship and Community Use

### Indigenous History (1600s–1774)

The Elk River valley's pre-contact Indigenous history remains partially documented. According to credible sources, **the Shawnee used the region as seasonal hunting grounds and held the river in such regard that their name for it translates to "river of plenty fat elk."** The **Delaware (Lenape) "had several autonomous settlements as far south as present-day Braxton County"** in addition to using the area as hunting territory. The **Mingo, who resided in the Tygart Valley and along the Ohio River in northern West Virginia, also used the Elk River area as hunting ground.**[^5][^7]

These three nations—Shawnee, Delaware, and Mingo—developed close associations and shared the Elk River valley despite linguistic differences (Shawnee and Delaware spoke Algonquian languages; Mingo spoke Iroquoian). However, after the **Treaty of Camp Charlotte (1774)**, the Shawnee were compelled to recognize the Ohio River as their southern boundary and relinquish all hunting claims to West Virginia and Kentucky. By the late 18th century, European settler expansion and subsequent Indian Removal policies effectively ended Indigenous presence in the region.[^6]

**Unverified:** Specific tribal ceremonies, harvesting practices, or sacred sites within the Elk River WMA area lack corroboration in accessible historical records and are not documented in this brief.

### 19th–21st Century Community Use

After Indigenous displacement, the Elk River valley became a frontier logging and farming region. **The Palmer Lumber Company's Holly River mill (established 1894) and Pardee and Curtin Lumber Company's extensive operations (1898–1924) transformed the landscape into a timber extraction economy, creating boom towns and railroad infrastructure that have largely disappeared.** Flooding remained a persistent threat; documented photographs show the **Elk River Bridge at Gassaway dangerously flooded ca. 1918.**[^21][^10][^11]

By the mid-20th century, recreational hunting and fishing became primary community uses. Today, **Elk River WMA serves as a vital hunting area for white-tailed deer, wild turkey, black bear, and numerous small game species, while Sutton Lake provides fishing (largemouth and smallmouth bass, muskie, walleye, trout) and boating recreation.**[^2][^22]

### Notable Events \& Restoration Efforts

- **Sutton Dam Flood Control Success:** Since its 1961 completion, Sutton Dam has **prevented over \$375 million in flood damages along the Elk, Kanawha, and Ohio Rivers**, fundamentally improving the safety and stability of downstream communities.[^17][^16]
- **Modern Elk Restoration (2015–Present):** Following the **1913 introduction of 50 Rocky Mountain elk to West Virginia** (which established a small, largely unsuccessful population), the state embarked on a new **active elk restoration program beginning in 2015.** Although the initial 1913 elk release in Pocahontas County did not result in a permanent population, **modern reintroduction efforts have been significantly more successful, with elk now present in West Virginia for the first time in over 140 years.**[^20][^19][^14]

## D. Historic vs. Current Ecology: Species Changes and Habitat Restoration

### Historic Ecology (Pre-1880)

The pre-industrial Elk River watershed was characterized by **diverse, mature hardwood forests (primarily northern hardwoods with red spruce at higher elevations) and a robust population of large fauna including elk, woodland bison, and gray wolves, all of which had been eliminated by overhunting prior to West Virginia statehood in 1863.** The river itself supported **naturally reproducing populations of trout in upper reaches and warm-water fish (particularly muskie) in lower sections**, with ecosystems regulated by seasonal Indigenous burning practices and natural disturbance regimes.[^5][^9]

### Industrial Decline (1880–1930)

**Between 1879 and 1920, approximately 30 billion board feet of timber were removed, with logging railroads penetrating previously inaccessible hollows, followed by uncontrolled wildfires that re-burned already-logged areas repeatedly.** This period resulted in:[^12]

- **Complete loss of old-growth forest** by approximately 1928[^9]
- **Severe soil erosion and water quality degradation**[^12]
- **Habitat loss for wildlife dependent on mature or old-growth forest structure**[^9]
- **Population collapse of wild turkey, deer, bear, and other game species**[^9]


### Modern Recovery \& Restoration (1945–Present)

- **Forest Maturation:** Serious forest protection began in the 1930s; by the 1975–1989 period, **saw timber inventories increased 34%, while seedling/sapling habitat decreased 62%, reflecting transition to mature, closed-canopy hardwood forest.** This benefits turkey, bear, and squirrel populations but disadvantages early-successional species (grouse, rabbits, songbirds).[^9]
- **Wild Turkey Restoration Success (1950–1989):** Following decades of unsuccessful farm-raising programs, **live-trapping and transplanting of wild turkeys began in 1950 (six birds at Coopers Rock) and accelerated in 1953, resulting in a full-time project that continued until 1989, when all suitable habitat in West Virginia was deemed occupied.** This program is considered one of America's most successful wildlife restoration efforts, attributed to both transplanting efforts and **natural forest maturation after the intensive logging period ended.**[^9]
- **Beaver Restoration (1930–present):** Following near-extinction, **beavers obtained from Michigan and Wisconsin were relocated to Tucker, Randolph, and Pocahontas counties beginning in 1930**, with descendants subsequently translocated throughout West Virginia, resulting in "scattered colonies all over the state."[^9]
- **Elk Restoration (2015–present):** In 2015, **West Virginia passed legislation establishing an Elk Management Area and tasking WVDNR with active elk restoration.** Over **44,000 acres of land (including 32,000 acres in a single acquisition) were permanently secured for wildlife management in state history**, with the **Tomblin Wildlife Management Area designated as the initial elk release site.** Modern reintroduction has proven successful, with **elk now breeding naturally and expanding range across the state.**[^19][^20][^14]
- **Ongoing Habitat Management:** The **WVDNR actively manages wildlife openings, develops trails, plants native species, and coordinates timber management to provide habitat diversity for multiple species**, moving beyond the mature-forest-only paradigm that had inadvertently harmed early-successional species.[^9]


### Current Ecological Status

Elk River WMA today represents a **mature hardwood forest in mid-to-late successional stage, with mixed age classes reflecting 90+ years of recovery post-logging.** The area supports **healthy populations of white-tailed deer, black bear, wild turkey, and numerous small game species, as well as diverse fish populations in Sutton Lake and river sections.** However, **invasive species and loss of early-successional habitat remain conservation concerns**, reflecting tension between fire-suppression policies and natural ecosystem function.[^22][^2][^9]
